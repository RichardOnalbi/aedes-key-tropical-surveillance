Core article concept

From documented spread to latent threat

Aedes albopictus showed that, when a vector invasion is detected late, the preventive window has already narrowed. At the same time, it left a methodological handbook: introduction routes, natural infection, vector metagenomics, and niche modelling.

This key follows that anticipatory logic. The proposal is to apply the lessons of Ae. albopictus proactively to non-native Aedes not yet established in Colombia, but already circulating nearby or along plausible entry corridors.

Interpretive framework

Ae. albopictus as a reference model

The case of Ae. albopictus allows two levels of interpretation to be separated: what has already been documented in Colombia and Latin America, and the transferable lessons for early surveillance. This distinction organizes the manuscript and justifies the taxonomic key as an operational tool rather than an ornamental appendix.

Prioritization criteria

How risk is ranked

The proposed prioritization combines geographic proximity, ecological compatibility, invasion history, vector relevance, and difficulty of detection. Under this framework, Ae. aegypti var. queenslandensis and Ae. vittatus appear as the most immediate threats, although by different routes.

Research agenda

Applying the albopictus handbook before establishment

Surveillance at entry routesPorts, used tires, ornamental plants, airport nodes, and the Darien corridor with focused trapping and COI barcoding as a minimum baseline.
Natural infectionDifferentiate rigorously among field evidence, molecular detection, and vector competence for prioritized pathogens.
Vector metagenomicsExplore the microbiome, virome, and coinfections without restricting surveillance to pathogen-specific panels.
Niche modellingAssess climatic and anthropogenic variables for tropical Colombia and compare the limitations of GARP, MaxEnt, and BioClim.
